01 · Motivation

Why flapping-wing flight?

1

Efficiency at small scale

Fixed and rotary wings lose efficiency at small scale (low Reynolds number) — flapping wings recover it, as insects and birds show.

Shyy et al. 2010, pp. 284–327

2

Propulsion + control, unified

Flapping wings unify propulsion and control in one structure — no separate rotors, ailerons, or rudder needed.

Chin & Lentink 2016, pp. 920–932

3

An open lab niche

Nobody in the lab currently works on this — today goes deep on control algorithms and hardware, from insect- to bird-scale robots.

02 · Hardware

The actuation challenge

Rotary motors and gearboxes don't shrink gracefully — friction and power density both work against you below a few grams. (Floreano & Wood 2015, pp. 460–466)

Motor + Transmission
  • Rotary DC / brushless motor
  • Gears, cranks, or linkages convert rotation to flapping / joint motion
  • Used by DelFly, Nano Hummingbird, Bat Bot, DelFly Nimble
Piezoelectric Direct-Drive
  • Ceramic (PZT) bimorph strain actuator
  • No motor, gears, or bearings
  • Only viable at sub-gram scale — RoboBee
Passive / Underactuated
  • Few actuators shape a compliant structure
  • Rest of the motion emerges passively
  • Bat Bot's membrane and PigeonBot's feathers
03 · Hardware

The control challenge

No room for ailerons, a rudder, or an elevator — the wing motion itself has to generate every control torque. (Chin & Lentink 2016, pp. 920–932)

Roll

Differential stroke amplitude or flapping frequency, left vs. right wing.

Pitch

Split-cycle / asymmetric-duration wing strokes, or shifting the stroke plane fore-aft.

Yaw

Asymmetric wing-root deflection or drag modulation via passive wing rotation.

Exception — DelFly II keeps a conventional tail (elevator + rudder), a bridge between fixed-wing and flapping-wing control.
04 · Software

A taxonomy of control architectures

Beyond WHAT is actuated: HOW is the motion computed and closed in feedback? Four tiers, in ascending sophistication. Click a tier.

TIER 0

Open-Loop Pattern Generation

A fixed or manually pre-set periodic waveform is commanded to the actuator(s); nothing sensed during flight shapes the motion.

e.g. PigeonBot's wrist/finger angles are commanded open-loop to reshape the wing. (Chang et al. 2020, art. eaay1246)
TIER 1

Passive Aeroelastic / Structural Self-Stabilization

A compliant structure (membrane, feather microstructure, flexure hinge) absorbs and redistributes aerodynamic loads with zero sensing or computation — "mechanical intelligence."

e.g. RoboBee's passive wing-pitch flexure (Ma et al. 2013); Bat Bot's silicone membrane (Ramezani et al. 2017).
TIER 2

Closed-Loop, Single-Loop Feedback

A sensed state (attitude, position) drives a PD/PID control law computed once per cycle.

e.g. RoboBee's 2013 PD attitude controller, fed by external motion capture rather than onboard sensing (Ma et al. 2013).
TIER 3

Cascaded, Multi-Loop Feedback (Inner + Outer)

A fast inner loop stabilizes body rates/attitude; a slower outer loop regulates position or velocity on top of it — the standard modern-autopilot pattern.

e.g. DelFly Nimble's Paparazzi-based cascaded PID (Karásek et al. 2018); Bat Bot's onboard IMU attitude loop (Ramezani et al. 2017).

06 · Framework

Defining a Control Complexity Index (CCI)

A four-axis rubric, each scored 1 (least complex) to 4 (most complex). CCI = sum of the four axes (range 4–16). Grounded directly in the Tier 0–3 taxonomy above.

1

Actuation Channels for Control

How many independently commandable inputs shape wing/control-surface motion. 1 = single channel · 4 = 5+ independent channels or high-DOF articulation.

2

Feedback Architecture

Tier 0–3 taxonomy position. 1 = Tier 0/1 · 4 = Tier 3, cascaded and autonomous.

3

Sensing & State-Estimation Burden

1 = none / external (teleoperation, mocap) · 4 = full multi-sensor autopilot suite (IMU + mag + baro + GPS).

4

Computational Autonomy

1 = off-board / teleoperated · 4 = onboard full autopilot stack (estimation + cascaded control + navigation).

09 · Synthesis

Cross-cutting themes

Fewer actuators, smarter structure

Bat Bot and PigeonBot trade actuator count for passive, compliant, or bio-derived structure that shapes itself. (Ramezani et al. 2017; Matloff et al. 2020)

Kinematics replace control surfaces

Every tailless robot here steers using stroke amplitude, timing, or wing-root deflection — not ailerons or a rudder. (Chin & Lentink 2016)

Complexity tracks design philosophy, not mass

CCI does not scale monotonically with mass or size — RoboBee (80 mg) and PigeonBot (280 g) both score below mid-mass DelFly Nimble, though the newest and smallest addition, the 1.29 g MIT Insect-Scale robot, now tops the whole set.

Sensing, feedback, and computation can be decoupled

RoboBee pairs a sophisticated feedback law with zero onboard autonomy; PigeonBot pairs the richest sensor suite with open-loop wing-shape control — complexity is multi-dimensional.

Synthesized from Chin & Lentink 2016, J. Exp. Biol. · Floreano & Wood 2015, Nature · robot deep-dive slides and CCI analysis.

Biological inspiration: morphing wings in swifts

  • Common swifts actively sweep their wings back and forward in flight.
  • High sweep → higher glide speed & efficiency; low sweep → higher maneuverability and turning.
  • Wing shape itself is a control and performance variable — not just passive structure.
  • Direct biological motivation for PigeonBot's and Bat Bot's underactuated, morphing-wing control strategies.

Lentink et al. 2007, pp. 1082–1085, Nature

10 · Outlook

Open challenges

  • Power density & endurance — sub-gram robots like RoboBee are still tethered for power.
  • Onboard sensing at insect scale — wing-beat vibration corrupts IMU signals; vision is heavy and power-hungry.
  • Robustness & damage tolerance — feathered and membrane wings must survive real aerodynamic loads and collisions.
  • Scaling up autonomy — several of these robots began teleoperated or externally tracked before gaining onboard control.
  • Closing the loop on morphing itself — PigeonBot's wing-shape control is still open-loop; no robot in this set senses and feeds back actual wing shape or aerodynamic load in real time.

Within the last few years, four new robots have each pushed a different one of these forward: power density and full autonomy on 26–95 mg-to-40 g platforms (Bee++, Aerobat, AirPulse), and onboard sensing plus learned control on a 1.29 g platform (the MIT Insect-Scale robot).

11 · Emerging Methods

Training algorithms for flapping-wing control

Why this is hard: flapping wings are deformable and aeroelastic, so the high-fidelity simulators that make sim-to-real easy for rigid quadrotors mostly don't exist here. All five approaches below lean on real-world data, imitation learning, or hybrid model-based/model-free architectures rather than pure simulated RL.

Quadrotor RL succeeded quickly in simulation because rigid-body dynamics are cheap and accurate to simulate; flapping wings couple structural deformation, unsteady aerodynamics, and fast body dynamics in ways that don't have an equivalently cheap high-fidelity simulator yet — hence the reliance on distillation, domain randomization, hybrid twins, and real sensor data above rather than training from scratch in sim.

12 · Emerging Methods

Learning directly from real animal flight footage

A distinct, emerging thread from the five approaches above: instead of training against simulated or expert-controller dynamics, these efforts train or benchmark directly against video of real birds and insects in free flight. Neither pipeline yet closes the loop on physical flapping-wing hardware — but as onboard compute gets cheaper, video-derived kinematics/imitation targets are a plausible sixth training approach.

Row 1 · Design benchmarking

Video → kinematics extraction

A markerless motion-tracking network (DeepLabCut) is run on video of freely-flying Canada geese and a frigatebird to automatically digitize wing points and reconstruct 3-D wingbeat trajectories. This quantifies stroke asymmetry and a ~19% swing in effective wing area across the stroke in both species — concrete kinematic benchmarks for bio-inspired MAV wing design, not yet a closed-loop controller.

Leontiuk, Kastalskiy, Khalid & Kazantsev 2026, Biomimetics 11(3), art. 212

Paper (open access, CC BY) ↗
Row 2 · Closed loop

Video → imitation-learned flight controller

An anatomically detailed Drosophila body, built from confocal-microscopy scans in MuJoCo, is driven by a neural-network controller trained with deep-RL imitation learning against 272 high-speed-video-tracked flight trajectories (~53 s) of real, freely-flying Drosophila hydei — reproducing real saccades and evasive turns from steering commands alone. Today this is a simulated musculoskeletal digital twin, not rigid hardware, but it demonstrates the full video-to-controller pipeline.

Vaxenburg et al. 2025, Nature 643, pp. 1312–1320

13 · Recommendation

Next steps: which design do we build?

Design path selection, actuation, and control — comparing two candidate reference designs to pick the most feasible path for a first build.

Option 1 · Published design

NWPU "Dove" — Biomimetic Flapping-Wing MAV

Yang, Wang & Song, Int. J. Micro Air Vehicles 10(1), 2018 ↗

  • Pigeon-scale vehicle: roughly 50 cm wingspan and about 220 g takeoff weight, sized for hand launch and outdoor missions.
  • Roughly 30 minutes of demonstrated flight endurance with an onboard autopilot, GPS waypoint navigation, and a micro camera.
  • Flexible membrane wings on carbon spars; passive wing deformation generates both lift and thrust each flap cycle.
  • Fully documented: mechanisms, wing structure, avionics, and flight-test results we can replicate directly.
  • Drive: one brushless DC motor through a gear reduction into a crank-rocker linkage, converting rotation into symmetric wing flapping at several hertz.
  • Throttle = thrust: flap frequency is the throttle — speeding up or slowing the motor commands climb, cruise, or descent.
  • Steering: a conventional bird-style tail driven by two micro servos handles pitch and yaw; no actuators in the wings themselves.
  • Stabilization: a small autopilot with an IMU closes attitude loops on the tail, filtering out flap-frequency oscillation in the sensor data.
Option 2 · State of the art

USTB Eagle-Like Flapping-Wing Drone

Global Times, Mar 19 2026 ↗ · CGTN flight footage ↗

256 min continuous flight — the current record for a flapping-wing drone. Previous record: 185.5 min, set in 2023 by an NWPU bionic aircraft.
  • One of four bird- and insect-inspired vehicles (eagle, pigeon, butterfly, beetle) built at the University of Science and Technology Beijing.
  • Eagle-inspired dual-lens vision: a telephoto plus short-focus camera pair recognizes and tracks vehicles, people, and buildings at multiple ranges, then locks onto static or moving targets.
  • Researchers flag the hard problems at this level: high-energy-density batteries, miniaturized precision flapping mechanisms, and wing materials that deform adaptively like real feathers.
  • No published design details — USTB has released capabilities only, not a replicable build.
Recommendation

Build on Option 1 (Dove)

Most feasible path: a documented, pigeon-scale design with hobby-grade actuators — while borrowing Option 2's endurance and vision ideas as stretch goals.

Why Dove wins on feasibility

  • Every subsystem is published and flight-proven; USTB released no design details, only capabilities.
  • Pigeon scale fits our budget, tools, and test space; 4-hour endurance demands battery and mechanism tech beyond a student build.
  • All parts are off-the-shelf: brushless motor, gears, micro servos, hobby autopilot.

Actuator architecture to build

  • Flapping drive: brushless outrunner + ~20:1 gear reduction into a symmetric crank-rocker four-bar, targeting 5–10 Hz flap rate.
  • Two sub-9 g micro servos on the tail: elevator for pitch, rudder/tail-twist for yaw and turns.
  • Single 2S–3S LiPo sized from bench power draw; motor throttle is the only thrust control.

Control approach

  • Hobby flight controller (e.g. F4-class) with IMU + GPS; PID attitude loops actuate the tail only.
  • Notch-filter the IMU at the flap frequency so the controller reacts to body motion, not wing beats.
  • Phase 1 manual RC flight; phase 2 stabilized; phase 3 GPS waypoints, like the Dove.

Next steps, in order

  • 01 CAD the crank-rocker mechanism and order motor, gears, servos, and flight controller.
  • 02 Bench-test the flapper on a stand: measure flap rate, thrust, and current draw.
  • 03 Fabricate carbon-spar membrane wings and balance the CG.
  • 04 Tethered hop tests, then manual RC flight, then close the stabilization loop.
14 · Wrap-up

Summary

  • Every flapping-wing robot answers two questions: how do you actuate the wing (hardware), and how do you compute and close the loop on its motion to generate torques (control software)?
  • Across mg-to-100s-of-g scales, designers trade actuator count, feedback architecture, sensing burden, and computational autonomy against each other — not against a single "complexity" dial.
  • The Control Complexity Index shows this trade-off is not simply a function of mass, size, or age — the newest and smallest addition (the 1.29 g MIT Insect-Scale robot) now scores highest of all ten, while the original smallest (RoboBee) and among the largest (PigeonBot) both score lower.
  • Bio-inspiration — feathers, bat membranes, insect wing hinges, butterfly undulation — is increasingly used to move control burden from actuators and computation into passive structure.
  • The field is moving fast: within the last few years, four new robots have each pushed a different open challenge forward — power density and full autonomy on 26–95 mg-to-40 g platforms, and onboard sensing plus learned control on a 1.29 g platform.
